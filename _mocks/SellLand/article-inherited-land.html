How to Sell Inherited Land: A Complete Guide

Inheriting land can be both a blessing and a burden. If you've decided to sell, this guide walks you through everything—from understanding probate to finding buyers and closing the deal.

Inheriting land often comes during one of life's most difficult times. While the property may have sentimental value, practical realities—distance, taxes, maintenance costs, disagreements with other heirs—can make selling the best decision for everyone involved.

If you've decided to sell inherited land, you're not alone. Our data shows that over 60% of inherited land is sold within 2 years of transfer. This guide will help you navigate the process efficiently and avoid common pitfalls.

1.Understanding Probate and Title Transfer

Before you can sell inherited land, you need clear legal ownership. This typically means going through probate—the legal process of validating a will and transferring assets to beneficiaries.

When Probate is Required

Most states require probate for real property transfers. The process varies significantly by state, but generally takes 6-12 months. Some states offer simplified probate for smaller estates.

Good to Know

If the deceased owned the land in a trust, probate may not be required. The successor trustee can typically sell the property directly. Check with the estate attorney handling the matter.

Getting Title in Your Name

Once probate is complete (or if you're the trustee of a trust), you'll need to record the deed transfer with the county where the land is located. This establishes you as the legal owner and gives you the right to sell.

Required documents typically include:

  • Death certificate of the deceased
  • Letters Testamentary or Letters of Administration (from probate court)
  • Executor's Deed or Trustee's Deed
  • Affidavit of Heirship (in some states)

2.Handling Multiple Heirs

If you inherited land along with siblings or other family members, you'll need everyone's agreement to sell. This is one of the most common complications—and one of the primary reasons people seek fast cash sales.

Getting Everyone to Agree

All legal owners must sign off on any sale. If one heir wants to keep the property and others want to sell, options include:

  • Buyout: One heir buys out the others' shares
  • Partition sale: A court-ordered sale if heirs can't agree
  • Negotiation: Finding terms everyone can accept

3.Tax Implications of Selling Inherited Land

Here's the good news: inherited property receives a "stepped-up basis," which can significantly reduce your capital gains tax liability.

Stepped-Up Basis Explained

When you inherit land, your cost basis for tax purposes is the fair market value at the time of the deceased's death—not what they originally paid for it. This means:

  • If your parent bought land for $5,000 in 1975
  • And it's worth $50,000 when they passed
  • Your basis is $50,000, not $5,000
  • If you sell for $52,000, you only pay tax on $2,000 gain
Important

The stepped-up basis is calculated at the date of death. If you wait years to sell and the property appreciates significantly, you'll owe capital gains on that appreciation. Selling while values are close to the inherited value can minimize taxes.

6.Common Issues and How to Solve Them

Back Taxes

If property taxes weren't paid before or after death, they become a lien on the property. Most cash investors will purchase land with back taxes—deducting the amount from the sale price—so this doesn't have to be a dealbreaker.

Unclear Title

Sometimes inherited land has title issues: old liens, boundary disputes, missing heirs, or improper transfers in the chain of ownership. A title company will identify these during the closing process. Experienced investors have resources to clear most title issues.

Land in Distant State

You don't need to visit or be physically present in the state where the land is located. Cash sales can be completed entirely remotely using electronic signatures and wire transfers.

Emotional Disagreements

Family land often carries emotional weight that complicates discussions about selling. It can help to focus on practical realities: ongoing taxes, maintenance needs, and the opportunity cost of holding an underutilized asset. Sometimes, converting the land to cash that can be divided fairly is the most equitable solution for everyone.
